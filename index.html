"Kindness Is Weird"

10 min • Warm-Up
Facilitator Script
So today's skill is literally called "loving-kindness" which ‚Äî I know. It sounds like a greeting card. But here's the thing: it's actually one of the oldest psychological technologies on earth. Buddhist monks have been training in this for 2,500 years, and brain scans show it physically changes the parts of your brain involved in empathy and emotional regulation. So it's less Hallmark, more mental athletics.
Before we get into it ‚Äî let's check in
Before we jump into the skill, I want to hear from everybody. Take a second and think about this:

Discussion Check-In

What's the kindest thing someone has done for you?

Doesn't have to be big. Sometimes the small ones stick with you more.

Give everyone a minute to think, then go around. No pressure to explain why it mattered ‚Äî just what happened.

Follow-up prompts if the group needs a nudge

"Did it surprise you? Like, was it someone you wouldn't have expected it from?"

"What about something kind you noticed between other people ‚Äî not even involving you?"

"Has anyone ever done something kind for you that you didn't appreciate until later?"

Purpose: This primes the affective state of warmth through recall rather than instruction. Hearing other people's stories builds group connection early. Let it breathe ‚Äî 2‚Äì3 minutes of sharing is great, more is fine if it's flowing.

What to listen for: Notice who shares something about being seen or noticed vs. something about being helped or rescued. That distinction often maps onto attachment style and can inform how you frame the rest of the session for different members.

If it's quiet: Go first. Share your own briefly, keep it small and ordinary. That sets the tone better than any prompt will.

The Four Wishes

10 min • Skill Teach
Facilitator Script
Here's how the skill works. Loving-kindness is basically directing four wishes toward someone. You start with someone easy ‚Äî someone you already care about, even a pet ‚Äî and then you expand outward to other people.

DBT Connection
In DBT, this lives in the mindfulness module. It practices observing ‚Äî you're noticing what it feels like to wish someone well. And it practices non-judgmental stance ‚Äî you're offering the wishes without deciding whether someone "deserves" them. It also works as opposite action when you're stuck in anger or shame, because the action urge in those emotions is to attack, and this is the behavioral opposite.
Key Insight
The cool part is you don't have to feel kind for it to work. It's a practice, not a feeling. You just do the reps.

Keep it brief and concrete. The skill teach should be efficient ‚Äî save the depth for the activity itself. Emphasize that this is more like a workout than a vibe. The "you don't have to feel kind" line lands well with skeptical teens.

Kindness Architects ‚Äî Build

25 min • Core Activity
Facilitator Script
You're going to be a Kindness Architect. Your job is to design a "Kindness Kit" for a specific person. Not yourself. I'm going to give you a scenario, and you're going to figure out: what does this person need to hear, see, or experience to feel the four wishes ‚Äî safe, happy, healthy, at ease?

Choose Your Scenario

A

A 14-year-old who just moved to a new school and eats lunch alone every day.

B

A 16-year-old whose best friend just ghosted them with no explanation.

C

A 15-year-old who keeps comparing themselves to people on social media and feels like they're falling behind at everything.

Design Your Kindness Kit

In each quadrant, sketch or write what you'd put in this person's kit. Objects, places, songs, a text message, a wish ‚Äî whatever. Get creative. No wrong answers.

Real Quick

Which quadrant was hardest to fill?

Why this works virtually: The design-challenge format gives teens something to do with their hands during screen time. The camera-hold share is low-risk because they're showing their paper, not their face/emotions.

Clinical data: The pattern of which quadrant teens find hardest to fill often maps directly onto their own unmet needs ‚Äî but you don't have to say that out loud.

Scenario design: The characters deliberately echo common ED-adjacent experiences (isolation, social comparison, relational rupture) without being explicitly about eating or bodies. This lets the material resonate without triggering content-specific defenses.

Timing: Give 10‚Äì12 minutes for building. Play chill background music. Check in casually at the halfway mark.

Closing Round

What's one thing from your Kindness Kit that you liked?

The intensity dial: The "gentle bridge" is deliberately understated. If a particular group has more capacity and trust, you can expand it. If they don't, you can skip the self-application entirely and still have taught the skill effectively through the design exercise. The learning is already in the doing.

For future sessions: You can build on this by having the group design a Kindness Kit for the "difficult person" category (someone they're angry at), which is where loving-kindness gets genuinely challenging and therapeutically powerful. Save that for a group with more cohesion.

End on creative energy: The round-robin closing keeps the final energy creative, generative, and outward-facing rather than introspective or heavy.
